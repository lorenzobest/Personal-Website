Early Life & Education

Childhood in Omaha

1930-1942

Warren Edward Buffett was born on August 30, 1930, in Omaha, Nebraska, the second of three children and the only son of Leila (née Stahl) and Congressman Howard Buffett. His father was a stockbroker and four-term Republican United States Representative.

Buffett displayed an interest in business and investing at a very young age. At age six, he purchased six-packs of Coca-Cola from his grandfather's grocery store for twenty-five cents and resold each bottle for a nickel, pocketing a five-cent profit.

Early Business Ventures

1943-1947

In his early teens, Buffett took his first steps into the world of high finance. At 11, he purchased three shares of Cities Service Preferred for himself and three for his sister Doris Buffett (at $38 per share). The stock quickly dropped to $27, but Buffett held on until it reached $40, when he sold his shares—only to watch Cities Service shoot up to nearly $200.

He worked in his grandfather's grocery store and delivered newspapers, meticulously recording his earnings for tax purposes and claiming a $35 deduction for his bicycle. By age 15, he was earning $175 monthly delivering Washington Post newspapers.

Education

1947-1951

Buffett wanted to skip college and go directly into business but was overruled by his father. He enrolled at the University of Pennsylvania's Wharton School but transferred to the University of Nebraska after two years, where he graduated at age 19 with a Bachelor of Science in Business Administration.

After being rejected by Harvard Business School, he enrolled at Columbia Business School after learning that Benjamin Graham (author of "The Intelligent Investor") and David Dodd taught there. He earned a Master of Science in Economics from Columbia in 1951.

Investment Career

1951-1954

Buffett-Falk & Co.

Worked as an investment salesman at his father's brokerage firm, Buffett-Falk & Co., in Omaha. During this time he also took a Dale Carnegie public speaking course.

1954-1956

Graham-Newman Corp.

Worked in New York City as a securities analyst at Graham-Newman Corp., the investment firm founded by his mentor Benjamin Graham.

1956-1969

Buffett Partnership Ltd.

Started his first investment partnership in Omaha with $105,100 from seven limited partners (family and friends). Over the next 13 years, the partnership generated an annual return of 29.5%, compared to 7.4% for the Dow Jones Industrial Average.

1965-Present

Berkshire Hathaway

Began acquiring shares in Berkshire Hathaway, a struggling textile manufacturing company, eventually taking control in 1965. He transformed it into a holding company for his investments, eventually phasing out the textile business to focus on insurance and other investments.

"It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price."

— Warren Buffett

Major Investments

Buffett is known for his value investing philosophy and his adherence to Benjamin Graham's principles. Some of his most notable investments include:

  • American Express: Invested after the 1963 salad oil scandal when shares were depressed
  • Geico: Bought the entire company in 1996 after first investing in 1951
  • Coca-Cola: Began purchasing in 1988, now one of Berkshire's largest holdings
  • Apple: Became one of Berkshire's largest holdings in the 2010s
  • BNSF Railway: Acquired in 2009 for $34 billion

Under Buffett's leadership, Berkshire Hathaway has grown into one of the largest and most respected companies in the world, with a market capitalization exceeding $700 billion.

Investment Philosophy

Value Investing Principles

Buffett's investment philosophy was heavily influenced by Benjamin Graham's value investing approach, though he has adapted it over time:

Margin of Safety

Only invest when there's a significant difference between intrinsic value and market price to allow room for error.

Economic Moats

Look for companies with sustainable competitive advantages that protect profits from competitors.

Long-Term Ownership

Hold investments indefinitely, focusing on companies that can compound value over decades.

Rationality Over Emotion

Make decisions based on fundamental analysis rather than market sentiment or short-term trends.

Circle of Competence

Only invest in businesses you thoroughly understand and can evaluate effectively.

Management Quality

Invest in companies run by honest, competent managers who think like owners.

"Rule No. 1: Never lose money. Rule No. 2: Never forget rule No. 1."

— Warren Buffett

Philanthropy & Legacy

The Giving Pledge

2010-Present

In 2010, Buffett and Bill Gates launched The Giving Pledge, committing billionaires to donate at least half their wealth to philanthropic causes. Buffett has pledged to give away 99% of his wealth.

As of 2023, he has donated over $50 billion, primarily to the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation and foundations run by his three children.

Berkshire Hathaway Shareholder Letters

Buffett's annual letters to Berkshire shareholders are considered essential reading for investors worldwide. They combine financial wisdom with wit and plainspoken advice, covering topics from investment strategy to corporate governance and life philosophy.

"Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."

— Warren Buffett
